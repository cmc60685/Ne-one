Security & Defense AI Policy Research Paper

Artificial Intelligence in Modern Military and Defense Strategies

A Comparative and Critical Analysis of Global Powers, Operational Realities, and Governance Imperatives

Rizwan Ullah & Hamza Saeed Toor National Defence University, Islamabad — Department of Leadership and Management Studies Supervised by Assoc. Prof. Dr. Ismail Ramay June 2026
Share this paper

Abstract

The integration of Artificial Intelligence into contemporary military doctrine represents the most consequential transformation in the conduct of organized violence since the advent of nuclear weapons. Unlike prior revolutions in military affairs, AI militarization operates across the full spectrum of conflict: from sub-threshold cyber operations and drone swarm warfare to high-intensity conventional strikes involving autonomous targeting at machine speed. This study conducts a comparative analysis of how the United States, China, Israel, India, Pakistan, and Iran are integrating AI into their defense architectures. Drawing on documented operational evidence from the U.S.-Israel-Iran campaign of 2026, the India-Pakistan conflict of May 2025, and Israel's sustained AI-enabled targeting operations in Gaza, the paper examines how AI is reshaping doctrines of warfare, altering balances of power, and posing unprecedented risks to deterrence stability. The study further evaluates strategic risks including algorithmic bias, accountability gaps, and the erosion of meaningful human control over lethal decision-making, and concludes with a critical assessment of the global governance landscape.

I. Introduction: The Third Offset and the Algorithmic Turn in Warfare

The integration of Artificial Intelligence into military doctrine constitutes what scholars of strategic studies increasingly describe as a third offset strategy — a deliberate technological leap designed to nullify adversarial advantages through superior cognitive automation. Where the first offset relied on nuclear deterrence and the second on precision-guided munitions and stealth technology, the third offset places machine learning, autonomous systems, and data-driven intelligence at the centre of military supremacy. This is not merely an incremental upgrade in warfare technology; it represents an epistemological rupture in the very logic of how states conceive, plan, and execute violence.

Carl von Clausewitz famously described war as the continuation of politics by other means. Today, one must add a critical amendment: increasingly, those means are algorithmic. The kill chain — the sequence of identify-locate-track-target-engage-assess — is being compressed from hours to seconds through machine inference. The United States military's campaign against Iran, launched under Operation Epic Fury in February 2026, demonstrated this compression in practice. According to U.S. Central Command, more than 1,000 targets were struck within the first 24 hours, nearly double the scale of the shock-and-awe campaign in Iraq in 2003. The primary instrument enabling this tempo was the Maven Smart System, a Palantir-developed AI platform that the Pentagon's Chief Digital and Artificial Intelligence Officer later described as processing 20 billion tokens per day at the peak of operations.

The global market for AI in military intelligence, combat systems, and battlefield decision-making was valued at approximately $9.13 billion in 2025 and is projected to reach $29.48 billion by 2035, reflecting a compound annual growth rate of approximately 12.5 percent. These figures, however, measure only declared and commercially visible investment. The true scale of AI militarization — including classified programs in China, covert systems in Israel, and dual-use civilian infrastructure in Iran — substantially exceeds any open-source market valuation.

II. Conceptual Framework: AI as a Revolution in Military Affairs

The concept of a Revolution in Military Affairs holds that periodic technological transformations fundamentally restructure the relationship between offense and defense, altering the relative advantages of states. AI represents the most consequential RMA candidate since nuclear weapons for one decisive reason: unlike nuclear deterrence, which functions through the threat of catastrophic mutual destruction and is thus self-limiting, AI militarization operates across the full spectrum of conflict without an inherent ceiling of deterrence.

AI military applications can be broadly organized across five operational domains. The first is Intelligence, Surveillance, and Reconnaissance, where machine learning dramatically accelerates the processing of satellite imagery, signals intelligence, and open-source data into actionable targeting products. The second is Autonomous Weapons Systems, ranging from loitering munitions to fully autonomous combat vehicles and drone swarms. The third is cyber warfare, where AI enables both offensive intrusion at machine speed and defensive anomaly detection that no human analyst could replicate. The fourth is logistics and predictive maintenance. The fifth, and most strategically consequential, is command-and-control, where AI-assisted decision support systems compress the cognitive burden on human commanders.

Key Concept: The Kill Chain

The kill chain refers to the military sequence of Identify-Locate-Track-Target-Engage-Assess. Traditionally requiring hours of human deliberation across command hierarchies, AI systems are now compressing this sequence to seconds. In Operation Epic Fury (2026), kill-chain expert Craig Jones confirmed that the Maven Smart System enabled targeting decisions "much quicker in some ways than the speed of thought." This compression is the defining operational reality of contemporary AI-enabled warfare.

III. The United States: Doctrine, Maven, and Operation Epic Fury (2026)

The United States currently represents the most institutionally advanced and operationally tested military AI ecosystem in the world. The Department of Defense's Joint All-Domain Command and Control initiative is the architectural expression of Washington's AI vision: a networked system integrating sensor data from land, sea, air, space, and cyberspace into a unified operational picture, enabling faster and more coordinated responses than any adversary can match. JADC2 is not merely a communications network; it is a cognitive architecture designed to achieve decision dominance.

The defining operational demonstration occurred during Operation Epic Fury, the U.S.-Israeli air and missile campaign against Iran beginning February 28, 2026. Over 38 days of combat operations, U.S. forces struck more than 13,000 targets across Iran. The cornerstone was the Maven Smart System, developed by Palantir Technologies under a $1.3 billion Pentagon contract. Maven fuses satellite imagery, drone feeds, radar data, and signals intelligence into a single interface, classifies targets, recommends weapons systems, and generates strike packages in near real time. Anthropic's Claude large language model was embedded within the system to summarize intelligence, analyze data, simulate scenarios, and generate semi-automated legal justifications for each strike.

However, the U.S. experience with AI targeting has not been without serious controversy. A Reuters investigation revealed that a U.S. airstrike in Iran struck a school, raising urgent questions about the reliability of AI-assisted target identification. Former military officials confirmed to Semafor that stale human-curated data fed into Maven contributed to the error, illustrating that algorithmic precision is only as reliable as the intelligence data on which it operates.

IV. China: Civil-Military Fusion and the Intelligentized Warfare Doctrine

China's approach to AI militarization is arguably the most strategically coherent and comprehensively resourced of any power in the current era. The People's Liberation Army has explicitly codified intelligentized warfare as the governing concept of its future military doctrine. Unlike the American model, China's Civil-Military Fusion policy mandates that civilian firms — including Alibaba, Baidu, Huawei, CETC, and NORINCO — directly contribute to military AI development, eliminating institutional friction and creating an efficient pipeline from commercial innovation to military application.

In a January 2026 broadcast, the PLA's National University of Defense Technology demonstrated a single soldier operating a coordinated formation of 200 autonomous drones — a capability the Pentagon has acknowledged it cannot currently match in speed or manufacturing scale. Researchers at Xi'an Technological University have reported that their DeepSeek-powered military planning system can analyze 10,000 battlefield scenarios in 48 seconds, compressing a process that would take human planners approximately two days. China's 15th Five-Year Plan institutionalizes Military-Civil Fusion as the primary mechanism for defense modernization, with national security rather than economic growth as the organizing principle.

V. Israel: The Operational Laboratory of AI-Enabled Targeting

If the United States represents AI doctrine and China represents strategic ambition, Israel represents real-world operational application at the highest intensity level currently documented in open-source literature. Israel's military-industrial complex, anchored by the IDF, Unit 8200, and defense firms including Elbit Systems and Rafael Advanced Defense Systems, has effectively transformed the country into a living laboratory for AI-enabled warfare.

The system designated Lavender, developed by Unit 8200, was designed to process mass surveillance data through a machine learning model trained to identify patterns correlating with militant activity. In the early weeks of the Gaza campaign, Lavender generated a list of approximately 37,000 potential human targets, described as low-level male Hamas sympathizers. Unit 8200 declared the system to be 90 percent accurate — meaning one in ten individuals flagged for lethal targeting may not have met the threshold of legitimate military targeting. The pace of AI-generated targeting recommendations created institutional pressure on human reviewers to approve strikes in intervals as brief as 20 seconds. The formal preservation of human-in-the-loop authorization thus became procedurally hollow.

The Lavender Precedent

Analysts at the Action on Armed Violence organization have warned that AI-driven targeting at scale with acknowledged error rates is being normalized. Gaza has functioned as Israel's AI weapons laboratory, with battle-tested systems now positioned for export to other states through established arms export channels.

VI. India and Pakistan: The South Asian AI Security Dilemma and the May 2025 Conflict

The May 2025 India-Pakistan military confrontation marked the first active combat use of drones between the two nuclear-armed states, bringing risks from theoretical concern to operational reality. A senior Indian military officer publicly acknowledged the use of an AI targeting system during Operation Sindoor, stating it achieved a 94 percent accuracy rate, analyzing real-time data from drones, radars, and satellite feeds combined with 26 years of archival intelligence data.

Pakistan's response demonstrated significant defensive AI capability. Pakistani integrated air defense systems successfully jammed, spoofed, and intercepted Indian drone incursions, with multiple sources confirming that Indian UAVs failed to reach their designated targets. Pakistan also deployed its own combat drones against 26 locations inside India in the early hours of May 10. The stabilization risks in South Asia are severe: both states maintain nuclear doctrines that rely on ambiguity, signaling, and deliberate communication — processes dependent on human interpretive judgment. AI systems introduced into early warning and cyber domains create new pathways for false positives and compressed decision cycles that represent a genuine threat to strategic stability.

VII. Iran: Asymmetric AI and Proxy Warfare in an Age of Sanctions

Iran presents a qualitatively different AI militarization model: the most advanced exemplar of asymmetric AI application, in which a mid-tier power with significant resource constraints achieves disproportionate strategic leverage through targeted AI investment in drone technology, cyber warfare, and proxy force coordination. Between 2022 and 2025, Iran supplied over 11,000 Shahed drones to Russia for deployment in Ukraine, providing Iranian engineers with an extraordinary volume of real-world performance data. In the 2026 U.S.-Israel-Iran conflict, Iran employed retaliatory cyber operations targeting AWS data centers in the UAE and Bahrain, demonstrating a sophisticated understanding of the infrastructure dependencies of AI-enabled warfare.

IX. Reshaping Doctrines: Autonomy, Speed, and the Erosion of Human Judgment

Across all cases examined, a common doctrinal transformation is visible: AI is compressing the decision cycle and creating structural pressure to remove or marginalize human judgment at critical nodes. The distinction between human-in-the-loop and human-on-the-loop captures this shift precisely. In the latter model, a human technically retains override authority but is presented with AI-generated recommendations at a pace and volume that renders genuine deliberation practically impossible. The human becomes a legitimating mechanism rather than a decision-making agent.

This doctrinal shift has consequences for deterrence theory that extend beyond the tactical level. Deterrence as developed by Thomas Schelling fundamentally presupposes rational, communicating, and temporally patient actors. AI systems are none of these things in the philosophical sense. They are optimizing rather than rational — a meaningful distinction. They do not communicate intent; they execute instructions. And they operate at computational speed that renders the signaling, pause, and recalibration on which deterrence stability depends practically infeasible.

X. Strategic Risks: Algorithmic Bias, Escalation, and Accountability

A. Algorithmic Bias

Algorithmic bias is the tendency of machine learning systems to reproduce and amplify the biases present in their training data. In military contexts, this manifests most directly in targeting and threat identification. The Lavender system's equation of being male with being a militant is one documented manifestation. Another is the acknowledged 10 percent error rate accepted as operationally tolerable — a threshold that would be considered catastrophic in any civilian risk management context.

B. Escalation Dynamics

The speed advantage that AI confers on military operations is simultaneously a crisis management liability. States that have not established shared understandings about the meaning of AI-enabled actions in ambiguous scenarios risk triggering unintended conflicts. The India-Pakistan crisis of May 2025 demonstrated that even between states with existing communication channels, the pace of AI-enabled operations can outrun the speed at which political authorities can intervene to arrest escalation.

C. Accountability Gaps

Traditional frameworks of military accountability rest on the principle of command responsibility. When an AI system generates targeting recommendations that prove to be based on faulty data, the chain of accountability becomes radically diffuse. In the Iran school strike case, former military officials attributed the error to stale human-curated data fed into Maven — simultaneously implicating human data managers, procurement officials, and the commanders who approved its operational use. This diffusion ensures that harm goes unaccountable, weakening the deterrent function of international humanitarian law.

D. The Capability-Intention Gap

This risk describes the tendency for adversaries to interpret AI-enabled military capabilities as preparations for a preemptive first-strike posture even when the deploying state's intentions are defensive — a classic security dilemma dynamic now operating at algorithmic speed.

XI. The Governance Imperative: Toward International Regulation of AI in Warfare

The global governance landscape for AI in military applications remains alarmingly underdeveloped relative to the pace of technological deployment. The United Nations Group of Governmental Experts on Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems has been deliberating since 2014 without producing a binding international instrument. The U.N. Secretary-General's New Agenda for Peace called for a legally binding treaty prohibiting lethal autonomous weapons systems from operating without human oversight, with a 2026 target completion date, but no such treaty has materialized.

Track-II dialogues between military and technical experts, shared incident reporting protocols to prevent AI-induced escalation, mutual declarations of human-in-the-loop requirements for nuclear command and control, and bilateral AI crisis communication channels represent incremental but practically achievable steps. The precedent of nuclear hotlines and pre-notification agreements during the Cold War demonstrates that adversarial states can develop limited technical cooperation mechanisms even in the absence of broader political convergence.

XII. Conclusion: Intelligence Without Wisdom

The Central Challenge

Intelligence, in the computational sense, is the capacity to process information and optimize for specified objectives. Wisdom, in the human sense, is the capacity to judge which objectives are worth pursuing and at what cost to which values. AI systems are capable of extraordinary intelligence. They are, by definition, incapable of wisdom — which is inseparable from moral consciousness, temporal perspective, and the recognition of one's own fallibility. The states examined in this study are in the process of delegating progressively greater portions of their lethal decision-making to systems that are intelligent without being wise.

The operational evidence of 2025 and 2026 has transformed the debate over AI in warfare from speculative to empirical. The Maven Smart System has been used to strike 13,000 targets in 38 days in Iran. AI-enabled drones have been used in active combat between two nuclear-armed states in South Asia. An AI targeting system has flagged 37,000 individuals for potential lethal action in Gaza with an acknowledged error rate and 20-second human review windows. These are not hypothetical scenarios; they are documented operational realities.

For emerging powers such as Pakistan, the strategic lesson is not to acquire AI military capability at any cost and under any ethical framework, but rather to recognize that the states which develop robust governance mechanisms alongside their AI capabilities will be better positioned to manage the stability risks that AI militarization inevitably generates. A nation's security is ultimately determined not only by the sophistication of its weapons but by the wisdom of the judgment that directs them.

Annex A: Key Terminology

Kill Chain
The military targeting sequence: Identify-Locate-Track-Target-Engage-Assess. AI is compressing each step from hours to seconds.
Intelligentized Warfare
China's PLA doctrine for integrating AI, big data, and autonomous systems as the primary basis of military power.
Civil-Military Fusion
China's policy mandating that civilian technology companies directly contribute to military AI development.
Autonomous Weapons Systems (AWS)
Weapons capable of selecting and engaging targets without real-time human direction.
JADC2
Joint All-Domain Command and Control: the U.S. military's AI-integrated system linking sensors and shooters across all operational domains.
Maven Smart System
Palantir's AI platform used by the U.S. military to fuse battlefield data and generate targeting recommendations.
Algorithmic Bias
Systematic errors in AI output caused by biases embedded in training data or model design.
Human-in-the-Loop
A system requiring genuine human cognitive engagement before a lethal decision is executed.
Human-on-the-Loop
A system that nominally retains human override authority but operates at a pace that renders genuine deliberation practically impossible.
Loitering Munitions
Unmanned aerial weapons designed to fly over a target area for extended periods before striking; also called suicide drones.
A2/AD
Anti-Access/Area Denial: a strategy using long-range precision weapons to prevent adversaries from projecting power into contested regions.
Normative Erosion
The gradual weakening of established ethical or legal standards through precedent-setting behavior.
